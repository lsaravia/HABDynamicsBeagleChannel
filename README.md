# High-frequency observations reveal contrasting HAB dynamics across two consecutive summers in the Beagle Channel, (Submitted) 

## Abstract
Harmful algal blooms (HABs) pose a recurrent threat to sub-Antarctic coastal ecosystems like the Beagle Channel. During the austral summer of 2022, an intense bloom of Alexandrium catenella (up to 5.0 × 10⁴ cells L⁻¹) was associated with mortality across marine and terrestrial food webs. Here, we extend the analysis of this event over a 15-month period (January 2021–April 2022) using high-frequency sampling to examine phytoplankton composition, multi-matrix toxin profiles (plankton, bivalves, and passive samplers), and concurrent oceanographic conditions. A. catenella was the dominant toxic species, producing paralytic shellfish toxins (PST) during two consecutive summers with markedly contrasting intensity. The 2021 event was moderate and spatially confined, whereas the 2022 bloom was widespread, with cell abundances and PST levels in plankton (up to ~3.7 × 10⁵ ng NT⁻¹) and bivalves (~1.9 × 10⁵ µg STXeq kg⁻¹) differing by more than two orders of magnitude between years. Crucially, we observed a pronounced spatial decoupling during the 2022 peak: planktonic PST levels differed by four orders of magnitude among adjacent stations, yet mussels reached uniformly high toxicities, highlighting the role of regional hydrodynamic connectivity. Additional toxin groups (DTX1, PTX2, SPX1, 20-Me-G, and domoic acid) were also detected, reflecting the coexistence of multiple toxigenic taxa. Generalized additive models (GAM)  identified water temperature, wind speed, ciliate abundance, SAM, and seasonality as significant predictors of A. catenella abundance. These findings demonstrate that HAB risk in complex sub-Antarctic systems cannot be assessed based on a single species or toxin, highlighting the critical need for high-resolution, multi-method monitoring frameworks.
